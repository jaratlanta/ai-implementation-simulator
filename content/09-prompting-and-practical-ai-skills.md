# Prompting & Practical AI Skills

## Why Prompting Matters

Prompting is the foundational skill of the AI era. Three reasons it matters above almost everything else:

**Clarity.** Better prompts create clearer, safer, and more accurate AI output. The quality of what comes out is directly proportional to the quality of what goes in.

**Efficiency.** Good prompting reduces rewrites and avoids hallucinations. A well-structured prompt can eliminate hours of back-and-forth iteration.

**Transferability.** Prompting habits transfer across every tool and use case. Whether using a general-purpose LLM, a specialized agent, or an enterprise AI platform, the same prompting disciplines apply.

## The Pilot Prompt Formula

Effective prompts follow a five-part structure:

**1. Role** — Who the AI should act as. Defining a role anchors the AI's perspective, vocabulary, and judgment. "Act as a public health communications strategist" produces fundamentally different output than a generic request.

**2. Context** — Audience, goals, constraints. This is the situational framing — who is this for, what do they already know, what are the boundaries? Context prevents the AI from making assumptions that lead to irrelevant or inappropriate output.

**3. Task** — Exactly what you want. Specificity is everything. "Write an update" is vague. "Draft a concise 3-paragraph briefing for senior executives addressing program performance, community reach, and gaps in expected outcomes" is actionable.

**4. Format** — Bullets, paragraphs, table, presentation content, etc. Specifying format ensures the output is immediately usable rather than requiring reformatting. This includes structural requirements like section headers, word counts, or specific frameworks.

**5. Review** — Human validation for content, tone, and safety. This is the human-in-the-loop step built directly into the prompting process. It serves as a reminder that AI output always requires human judgment before deployment.

## Bad Prompt vs. Better Prompt

A bad prompt: "Write an update on our programs and what kind of impact we're having."

A better prompt: "[ROLE] Act as a public health communications strategist at a nonprofit foundation. [CONTEXT] We have a summary of last month's key program metrics and field reports across our active health initiatives. [TASK] Draft a concise 3-paragraph briefing for senior executives and board members addressing program performance, community reach, and any gaps in expected outcomes. [FORMAT] Highlight 2 material variances from targets and 1 emerging risk or opportunity leaders should monitor heading into next quarter."

The difference is not just length — it is precision. The better prompt eliminates ambiguity, specifies the audience, defines the format, and ensures the output is actionable.

## Advanced Prompting Techniques

### Thinking Mode

When dealing with complex analysis, research, or multi-step reasoning, switching to "thinking" mode (available in advanced LLMs) produces dramatically better results. Thinking mode causes the AI to plan its approach, evaluate its reasoning, and produce more nuanced output. This is particularly valuable for research reports, strategic analysis, and data-driven recommendations where shallow answers are dangerous.

### Iterative Prompting

Complex outputs are rarely achieved in a single prompt. The most effective approach is iterative — building output in stages. Start with a broad framework, then refine specific sections, then ask for critique and improvement. Each iteration adds depth and precision that a single massive prompt cannot achieve.

### Source Citation

For any output that will inform decisions or be shared externally, explicitly request source citations. This creates accountability in the AI's output and provides a verification path for human reviewers. Even with citations, clicking through and verifying is essential — but having citations dramatically speeds up the review process.

### Structured Output Requests

When AI output needs to feed into another process — a presentation, a report template, a database — specifying the exact structure saves enormous time. Requesting tables with specific columns, content organized by specific sections, or output formatted for specific platforms eliminates the reformatting step entirely.

## Golden Rules of AI Governance (Individual Level)

Three foundational governance rules that every individual in an organization should follow:

**1. Use Company-Approved Accounts.** Sign in with work email or SSO. Draft and store AI outputs within approved enterprise platforms. Personal accounts and consumer tools should not be used for organizational work.

**2. Don't Feed the Robots Your Secrets.** No confidential data, private files, or client information should be entered into AI tools unless the security architecture explicitly supports it. If information is under NDA, it stays offline or goes only into approved air-gapped systems.

**3. Pay for Personal Accounts.** For personal AI use, upgrade to paid tiers that keep data private. Prompts and outputs on paid enterprise plans are not used for model training. In consumer settings, explicitly turn off chat training in data control settings.

## What Makes a Good AI Use Case

Four criteria for evaluating whether an AI use case is worth pursuing:

**High-volume, repetitive tasks.** AI handles volume and repetition with speed and consistency, freeing people to focus on higher-value work. If a task is done frequently and follows a pattern, it is a strong candidate for AI.

**Decisions based on patterns or data.** Leveraging AI for data-driven decisions ensures actions are informed by the full breadth of available information rather than individual judgment alone.

**Clear business value or ROI.** Every AI use case must demonstrate clear business value. If the value cannot be articulated, the use case should be questioned or refined until it can.

**Fast feedback loops.** The best AI use cases enable rapid learning and continuous engagement. If the output can be evaluated quickly and the system can be adjusted based on that evaluation, the use case will improve over time.

## The Immersion Workshop Model

The workshop structure is designed to move participants from passive learning to active creation within a single day. The morning session covers foundations (AI 101, industry trends, golden rules, and prompting basics) combined with an immersion challenge where teams apply what they've learned to a creative scenario.

The afternoon session shifts to practical application — implementation planning, change management, a second immersion challenge focused on real organizational use cases, and a strategy lab where teams develop actual AI strategies using the frameworks taught earlier in the day.

The immersion challenges are deliberately designed to be engaging and slightly unexpected (designing a food truck concept using AI, for example) to lower psychological barriers and demonstrate that AI is approachable and even fun. The creative exercise builds confidence and prompting fluency before participants tackle their own organizational challenges.

By the end of the day, participants have not just learned about AI — they have used it to create actual deliverables, developed use cases for their own roles, and built the beginning of an organizational AI strategy. The goal is that every participant walks away with usable knowledge, practical skills, and genuine inspiration.

## AI Archetypes

Different people naturally gravitate toward different relationships with AI. Understanding these archetypes helps organizations design adoption strategies that meet people where they are rather than forcing a one-size-fits-all approach. Some people are natural experimenters who dive in immediately; others are cautious evaluators who need to see proof before committing; still others are systematic builders who want to understand the architecture before touching the tools. Effective AI adoption acknowledges and accommodates all of these approaches.
