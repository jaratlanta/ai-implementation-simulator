## Core Insight
Instead of asking clients to document processes separately (which routinely fails due to time constraints, competing priorities, and documentation fatigue), co-create the discovery documentation in structured working sessions. This accelerates pain-point identification, builds genuine shared understanding, and creates ownership of the problem definition before any solution design begins. The client sees their process articulated clearly for the first time; the consulting team builds empathy and specificity that can't be generated from homework assignments.

## Context
Discovery assignments typically fail at the documentation stage. Clients receive requests to "document your current process" and are overwhelmed: this requires setting aside hours, involves multiple stakeholders, and feels like extra work stacked on already-busy schedules. Documentation gets postponed, filled out hastily, or delegated to junior staff who don't understand the full complexity. The result is incomplete, inaccurate documentation that misses critical pain points.

Working session discovery flips this dynamic. Instead of asking clients to do the documentation, the consultant and client do it together in structured 2-4 hour sessions. The consultant asks specific, probing questions. The client (process owners, operators, department heads) provides detailed context in real time. Documentation emerges through dialogue, not through isolated homework.

This approach accelerated discovery timelines by 60-70% on average and significantly improved problem definition quality because the actual problem owners were in the room explaining their work, not trying to write it down under time pressure.

## Detail

### Why Homework-Based Discovery Fails

**Challenge 1: Competing Priorities**
Process owners are busy. They have actual jobs. Asking them to "spend 4 hours documenting your current workflow" gets deprioritized by real work. Two weeks pass, documentation is incomplete, and the consultant has to follow up. Now two weeks are lost.

**Challenge 2: Documentation Fatigue**
Writing documentation is tedious. Even if a process owner allocates time, the work is draining and low-reward. They write down 30% of what they know, stop, and submit what they have. The consultant then spends weeks trying to fill gaps through follow-up questions.

**Challenge 3: Loss of Context**
When documentation is created in isolation, context is lost. A process owner might write "we run reports twice weekly" but miss the crucial detail: one report triggers escalation protocols that only happen 15% of the time, and those 15% consume 60% of the team's crisis management time. When a consultant only reads the homework, they miss these non-obvious insights.

**Challenge 4: Misalignment on Detail Level**
Clients often document at the wrong level of detail. Either they're too high-level (missing critical edge cases and failure modes) or too detailed (documenting every micro-step without distinguishing signal from noise). The consultant and client have different assumptions about what "documented" means.

**Challenge 5: No Shared Understanding**
Homework-based discovery creates a dead artifact: a document the client submitted that the consultant reads. There's no real-time discussion, no "aha moments," no moment where the client themselves realizes something important about their own process. Shared understanding never crystallizes.

### Structure of Working Sessions

Successful working sessions follow a tight structure to keep energy high and output focused.

**Pre-Session Setup** (consultant's responsibility)
- Schedule 2-4 hour sessions with 4-8 key stakeholders (mix process owners, operators, supervisors, maybe one frontline staff)
- Send a brief agenda (e.g., "we'll walk through your weekly scheduling process, understand constraints, and identify the top 3 pain points")
- Ask participants to bring any artifacts they use: spreadsheets, templates, old reports, screenshots of systems they work in
- Set expectations: "This is a conversation, not a presentation. We want to understand how your work actually happens."

**Opening (10-15 minutes)**
- Introduce the process: "We're going to walk through your [process]. We'll go slow, ask lots of questions, and document what we learn so we all have the same understanding."
- Establish that there are no wrong answers: "We're here to learn how the work actually gets done, not to judge how it's currently done."
- Explain the output: "By the end, we'll have a visual map of the process and a list of the biggest friction points. That's the input for any improvements we discuss."

**Deep Dive (90-120 minutes)**
- Start with the happy path: "Walk me through a typical [process instance]. You just received [trigger]. What happens next?"
- Document in real time (whiteboard, visual diagram, or shared digital canvas that everyone can see)
- Ask probing questions: "Who approves this?" "What if X happens instead?" "How often does that exception occur?" "What bothers you most about that step?"
- Let the process owner(s) correct and refine: "Is that accurate? Did I miss anything?"
- Identify edge cases and exceptions: "In the last month, when did this process not go as planned?"
- Capture pain points as they emerge: "That sounds frustrating. How much time does that usually take?" "What would an ideal solution look like?"

**Synthesis (20-30 minutes)**
- Wrap up by reviewing what you've documented: "Here's the process as I understand it. Here are the pain points we identified. Does this match what you experience?"
- Ask for final corrections
- Ask: "If you could fix one thing about this process, what would it be?" (This prioritizes problems by real pain, not by consultant hypothesis.)

**Output**
- Leave the session with a working document that everyone has seen and agreed to
- Usually a combination of: visual process map, written descriptions of key steps, list of identified pain points with frequency/impact assessment
- Send this back to the client within 24 hours for final review (they'll catch things they forgot to mention in the room)

### How Co-Creation Builds Buy-In

Working session discovery creates buy-in at multiple levels:

**Level 1: Ownership of Problem Definition**
When a process owner articulates their own pain in front of consultants and peers, they become personally invested in the problem being solved. This isn't a consultant-identified problem; it's *their* problem, stated in their language. When you eventually propose solutions, they're evaluating solutions to *their* problem, not implementing the consultant's thesis.

**Level 2: Visibility to Other Stakeholders**
In a working session, department heads and supervisors hear directly from process owners about specific challenges they didn't know about or underestimated. "I spend three hours weekly pulling data from three systems and manually reconciling it"—said in front of leadership—becomes a priority. This wouldn't have surfaced in individual homework submissions.

**Level 3: Empathy and Credibility**
Consultants who sit with process owners, listen carefully, ask smart follow-up questions, and document what they hear build credibility through empathy. The consultant isn't treating the current process as obviously flawed; they're treating it as the result of real constraints and judgment. This respect becomes the foundation for difficult conversations later.

**Level 4: Shared Vocabulary**
By the end of a working session, everyone uses the same language to describe the process and its problems. "The reconciliation nightmare" becomes a shared reference point. This prevents the consultant from later proposing solutions to the wrong problem or using language that doesn't land.

**Level 5: Reduced Scope Creep**
When stakeholders see a prioritized list of pain points that emerged from their own conversation, it's easier to say "let's focus on these top 3 and not boil the ocean." Scope feels negotiated, not imposed.

### Relationship to Shadowing Methodology

Working session discovery complements (not replaces) shadowing for process discovery.

**Shadowing**: Consultant observes the actual work happening in real time. Shows what people actually do (not what they think they do or what documentation says they do). Reveals hidden workarounds, unofficial steps, and true time allocation.

**Working Sessions**: Consultant and stakeholders discuss the process together, articulate pain points, and co-create documentation. Shows how people think about their work and what they identify as problems.

Both methods are valuable, but they answer different questions:
- Shadowing answers: "What is actually happening?"
- Working sessions answer: "Why does the work happen this way, and what bothers people about it?"

**Best Practice**: Combine them in sequence. Working sessions first (faster, builds rapport) to identify pain points and prioritize areas for deep understanding. Then shadowing of the highest-pain activities to see what's really happening. Then another working session to validate observations and iterate on solutions.

This sequence: (1) establishes shared understanding, (2) reveals reality, (3) validates improvements against reality.

### Relationship to "Eliminate Monotony First"

A related principle: when gathering problem statements in discovery, prioritize pain that reduces monotony, stress, and cognitive load—not just efficiency.

Working sessions are excellent for surfacing this type of pain. In a one-on-one homework submission, a process owner might not mention "pulling data from three systems is so boring that I make mistakes." In a group working session, someone says it out loud, and you see nods around the table. That's real pain, not stated as business value but deeply felt.

This has important implications: solutions that reduce monotony and stress will have higher adoption and satisfaction, even if they don't improve efficiency dramatically. A 10% efficiency gain that feels like drudgery gets resisted. A 5% efficiency gain that eliminates repetitive manual work gets enthusiastically adopted.

## Application Guide

**Step 1: Prepare Session Participants**
- Identify 4-8 key stakeholders who understand the process deeply: process owners, department heads, supervisors, maybe 1-2 frontline staff
- Schedule back-to-back 2-hour sessions for maximum momentum (easier than spreading across multiple weeks)
- Confirm they'll have uninterrupted time (block their calendar, mute Slack, close email)
- Ask them to bring artifacts: templates, screenshots, samples of outputs, previous documentation

**Step 2: Prepare Materials**
- Create a shared visual canvas (whiteboard if in-person, Miro/Mural/Figma if remote; many teams use a live Google Doc)
- Prepare simple templates: process maps, pain point trackers, priority matrices
- Have facilitators ready with probing questions based on pre-meeting research

**Step 3: Run First Session**
- Facilitate the deep dive: walk through the process, document in real time, ask specific follow-up questions
- Capture pain points as they emerge, not as separate "issues" list—they're part of understanding the process
- Be precise about language: push back on vague statements ("It's complicated") with specific questions ("Walk me through the last time this went wrong")

**Step 4: Synthesize and Share**
- Within 24 hours, send back a polished version of the working document for final review
- Ask: "Did I miss anything? Is this accurate?"
- Incorporate final feedback

**Step 5: (Optional) Shadow the Highest-Pain Activities**
- If time allows, observe 4-6 hours of the work that the team identified as most painful
- Note what's actually happening vs. how it was described in the session
- Validate observations with the team

**Step 6: Second Working Session (Optional But Recommended)**
- 1-2 weeks later, reconvene to discuss what you learned from shadowing (if applicable)
- Refine problem statements and pain point prioritization
- Begin exploring potential solutions collaboratively

**Step 7: Problem Statement Finalization**
- Collaboratively write final problem statements for the top 3-5 pain points
- Each statement should include: what's happening, why it's a problem, who it affects, and rough priority
- This becomes the input for solution design

**Timing expectation**: End-to-end discovery (working sessions + optional shadowing + second session) typically takes 2-3 weeks. That's 60-70% faster than homework-based discovery.

## Key Takeaways
- Homework-based discovery fails because it overloads process owners with documentation work and misses context that only surfaces in dialogue. Working sessions dramatically accelerate discovery while improving problem definition quality.
- Co-created discovery documentation builds ownership, surfaces hidden pain points, creates shared vocabulary, and prevents scope creep. The client sees their process articulated clearly for the first time.
- Working sessions are tightly structured: opening → deep dive with real-time documentation and probing questions → synthesis. The output is a validated, visual map of the process and prioritized pain points.
- Combine working sessions with shadowing for maximum insight: sessions for understanding and pain identification, shadowing for reality checking.
- Pain that reduces monotony, stress, and cognitive load is as important as efficiency metrics in discovery. Working sessions surface this type of pain more effectively than homework submissions.
