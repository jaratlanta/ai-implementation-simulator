## Core Insight
Move away from per-unit pricing, hourly billing, and retainer structures for compressed, intensive engagements with clear output deadlines. Instead use tiered fixed-fee structures (Focused / Recommended / Strategic) that signal seriousness, eliminate price negotiation friction, and create clear value differentiation. This works especially well for time-boxed prototype-driven engagements (4-8 weeks) where outcomes are measurable and scoping is tight. Fixed pricing shifts the conversation from "how much does this cost?" to "which tier of work matches our ambition?"

## Context
Traditional pricing models create friction in shorter engagements:

- **Hourly billing**: Client fears scope creep and unbounded costs. Consultant fears underbidding. Both parties are locked in a cost-minimization negotiation rather than value conversation.
- **Per-unit pricing**: Works for repeatable deliverables but misaligns with intensive, custom work. "What do you mean this prototype costs the same as a standard deliverable?"
- **Retainer structures**: Designed for ongoing work, not compressed projects. A 4-week prototype project doesn't fit retainer economics.

Tiered fixed-fee pricing emerged as a better model for project work: it removes cost uncertainty (fixed price), creates clear differentiation between "good enough" and "transformative" (tiers), and enables faster sales cycles (choose a tier, not years of negotiation).

This approach works best for engagements that are:
- Time-boxed (4-12 weeks, defined end date)
- Output-focused (prototype, strategy, analysis, not "we'll help you figure it out")
- Scope-definable (you can clearly articulate what's included in each tier)
- Value-obvious (client can assess if they got ROI within 30 days of completion)

## Detail

### Why Per-Unit Pricing Undermines Value Perception

Per-unit pricing (charged per deliverable, per analysis, per prototype feature) creates several problems in higher-touch engagements:

**Problem 1: False Commodity Feel**
When you charge by unit, it implies units are interchangeable. "A prototype costs $X." But a prototype for a board retreat (prototype-driven-board-engagement) is different from a prototype for internal testing. Per-unit pricing erases those differences and commoditizes the work.

**Problem 2: Misaligned Incentives**
Per-unit pricing incentivizes the consultant to maximize units, not maximize value. "Should we add this variation to the prototype?" Under per-unit pricing, the consultant benefits from more units. Under tiered pricing, the consultant benefits from choosing the right tier for the client's ambition.

**Problem 3: Client Ambiguity**
When a client sees "prototype: $20k," they don't know if that's the right scale. Is that assuming one scenario or three? Does it include data integration? Is that the minimum viable prototype or the go-to-market version? Ambiguity kills deals.

**Problem 4: Negotiation Friction**
Per-unit pricing invites negotiation: "Can we get it for $15k instead?" With tiered pricing, you either choose Focused tier ($15k) or you don't. The choice is transparent.

**Problem 5: Underprices Intensive Work**
If a prototype requires 8 weeks of intensive work with a small team, but a simpler prototype takes 3 weeks, per-unit pricing forces you to charge the same (because they're both "a prototype"). You either underprice the complex one or overprice the simple one. Tiers let you differentiate based on scope and intensity.

### How Tiers Create Anchoring

Psychological anchoring is powerful in pricing. When a client sees three options, they anchor to the middle option (Recommended) and evaluate the others relative to it.

**Tier Structure Example**: (for a 6-week prototype-driven engagement)

**Focused Tier - $35k**
- Prototype focused on one primary use case
- Data integration with one data source
- Real-time modification capability for 3-5 parameter variations
- 2 working sessions with the client team
- Deliverable: working prototype + technical documentation
- Timeline: 6 weeks
- Best for: clarity and quick proof of concept

**Recommended Tier - $65k**
- Prototype covers primary use case + 2 secondary use cases
- Data integration with 2-3 data sources, cross-system reconciliation
- Real-time modification for 7-10 variations (more complex scenarios)
- 4 working sessions including board retreat facilitation
- Deliverable: working prototype + technical documentation + 1-hour board training
- Timeline: 6 weeks
- Best for: executive buy-in and deeper exploration

**Strategic Tier - $120k**
- Prototype covers primary + 3-4 secondary use cases
- Data integration with 3-5 data sources, complex reconciliation logic
- Real-time modification for 15+ variations (full scenario flexibility)
- 6 working sessions, including interactive board retreat facilitation with live modification
- Deliverable: working prototype + comprehensive technical + architectural documentation + post-retreat coaching (2 sessions)
- Timeline: 8 weeks
- Best for: organization-wide strategic alignment and innovation sprint outcomes

**The Anchor Effect:**
- Focused ($35k) looks like a safe, minimal option
- Recommended ($65k) becomes the "right" choice for most clients (it's the psychological default)
- Strategic ($120k) makes Recommended feel like a bargain

The gaps are intentional: 2x from Focused to Recommended, ~2x from Recommended to Strategic. This signals that the scope and ambition actually increase.

### What Goes in Each Tier

Tiers should vary across multiple dimensions, not just price:

**Scope/Complexity**:
- Focused: narrow use case, single data source, 3-5 scenarios
- Recommended: 2-3 use cases, 2-3 data sources, 7-10 scenarios
- Strategic: broad use cases, complex integrations, 15+ scenarios

**Intensity of Engagement**:
- Focused: 2 working sessions, written feedback
- Recommended: 4 sessions, interactive board facilitation
- Strategic: 6 sessions, board facilitation, post-engagement coaching

**Documentation and Deliverables**:
- Focused: working prototype + basic technical documentation
- Recommended: prototype + technical docs + board training materials
- Strategic: prototype + comprehensive technical docs + architectural guidance + coaching

**Timeline**:
- Focused: 6 weeks (team of 2-3)
- Recommended: 6 weeks (team of 3-4)
- Strategic: 8 weeks (team of 4-5, more complex data work, deeper coaching)

**Decision Support**:
- Focused: client sees what's possible
- Recommended: client understands tradeoffs and sees implementation pathways
- Strategic: client is ready to make strategic decisions about the work

The key: each tier should feel like a distinct level of commitment, not just "more of the same thing."

### How to Scope Time-Boxed Prototype Engagements

Tight scoping is critical for fixed-fee pricing. If you're wrong about scope, your margin disappears.

**Practice 1: Ruthless Use Case Prioritization**
Don't try to cover 10 use cases. Identify the 3 that, if solved, unlock the most value or remove the biggest skepticism. Focus there. "We can address the others in phase 2" is an honest conversation.

**Practice 2: Data Source Limits**
Each data source adds complexity (integration, reconciliation, transformation, quality issues). Limit to 2-3. If a client needs 5 data sources, that's a signal the tier should be Strategic, not Focused.

**Practice 3: Scenario Bounding**
Don't offer unlimited scenarios. Define the variation space: "We'll handle variations in facility size (small/medium/large), staffing levels (full/reduced), and one custom parameter you choose." That's bounded and feasible in 6 weeks.

**Practice 4: Documentation Scoping**
Be specific about what "documentation" means. "Technical documentation" might be 5 pages (architecture + key algorithms) or 50 pages (line-by-line implementation notes). Define it.

**Practice 5: Engagement Scoping**
"Working sessions" varies wildly. Define it: "4 working sessions, 2 hours each, with 6-8 participants per session, addressing the primary use cases."

**Practice 6: Success Scoping**
Define what "done" looks like. "We've delivered a working prototype that demonstrates the capability and passes user testing with the 8-person core team" is clear. "We've transformed how the organization thinks about this problem" is not.

### When Fixed-Fee Beats Hourly/Retainer

**Fixed-Fee Advantage Scenarios:**
- Client wants a specific deliverable with a clear deadline (prototype, analysis, strategy document)
- You can define scope clearly upfront
- Timeline is 4-12 weeks (long enough to absorb small unknowns, short enough that surprises are rare)
- Client wants cost certainty and won't tolerate "hours + expenses"
- Sales cycles are fast and client wants to move immediately

**Retainer/Hourly Advantage Scenarios:**
- Work is genuinely ongoing (support, maintenance, continuous optimization)
- Scope can't be defined upfront (discovery-driven work where you're figuring out the problem as you go)
- Client is experimenting and wants to adjust based on learnings
- Timeline is open-ended
- Work is unpredictable in effort

Most prototype-driven engagements (4-8 weeks, clear output, focused scope) are ideal for fixed-fee. Most continuous engagements (ongoing optimization, evolving needs) are ideal for retainer.

### Pricing Reality Check

Tiered fixed-fee pricing only works if you can deliver each tier profitably. Work backward from pricing:

**Example Assumptions**:
- Focused tier cost to deliver: $25k (3 people × 6 weeks × $40/hour blended rate)
- Focused tier price: $35k → 40% margin
- Recommended tier cost: $45k (4 people × 6 weeks, slightly higher blended rate)
- Recommended tier price: $65k → 44% margin
- Strategic tier cost: $85k (5 people × 8 weeks)
- Strategic tier price: $120k → 41% margin

Margins should be consistent across tiers (40-45% is healthy for professional services). If a tier's margin is below 30%, you're underpricing or overscoping.

If you can't deliver a tier profitably, don't offer it. It's better to have two solid tiers than three where one is a margin killer.

## Application Guide

**Step 1: Define Your Engagement Model**
- What type of projects do you do? (prototypes, strategies, implementations, transformations)
- What's your standard timeline? (typically 4-12 weeks)
- What's your team structure? (small elite team, medium team, large team)
- What's your cost structure? (burdened labor costs per person per week)

**Step 2: Design Three Tiers**
- Sketch what "Focused," "Recommended," and "Strategic" mean for your engagement type
- Vary scope, complexity, timeline, deliverables, and engagement depth
- Ensure each tier is meaningfully different, not just "more stuff"
- Rough price each tier based on cost to deliver + desired margin (40-45%)

**Step 3: Test Tier Pricing with 3-5 Recent Engagements**
- Look at recent projects. What tier would each have been?
- Did you deliver it profitably at that price point?
- Adjust pricing based on what you learn

**Step 4: Create Tier Descriptions**
- Write a clear, single-paragraph description of each tier
- Include scope, timeline, deliverables, and best-use case
- Make each sound attractive for different reasons (Focused = fast, Recommended = balanced, Strategic = transformative)

**Step 5: Train Your Sales Team**
- Explain the psychology of tiering: clients will anchor to Recommended
- Teach reps not to present pricing upfront: first, understand client ambition, then recommend a tier
- "What's your timeline and budget?" should be replaced with "What are you trying to accomplish and what decisions need to happen by when?"

**Step 6: Use Tiering in Sales Conversations**
- Early: "We offer three levels of engagement. Let me describe them and we can see which matches your needs."
- After discovery: "Based on what I'm hearing, I'd recommend the Strategic tier because [reasons]. Focused would work if [conditions]. Let's talk through tradeoffs."
- Never: "Here's a proposal at $120k." Always: "Here are three options at $35k, $65k, and $120k. Which resonates with your ambition?"

**Step 7: Monitor and Adjust**
- After each project, capture: actual costs, profitability, client satisfaction, scope creep incidents
- Every 6 months, revisit pricing. Are tiers profitable? Are sales teams using them effectively? Do clients understand what they're getting?

## Key Takeaways
- Per-unit pricing commoditizes complex work and creates negotiation friction. Tiered fixed-fee pricing signals value differentiation and enables faster sales cycles.
- Three tiers (Focused / Recommended / Strategic) create anchoring: clients gravitate to Recommended, which looks like the balanced choice. Focused and Strategic frame it.
- Each tier should vary across multiple dimensions: scope, complexity, intensity, timeline, deliverables, engagement depth. Don't just charge more for "more stuff."
- Fixed-fee pricing requires ruthless scoping: prioritize use cases, limit data sources, bound scenarios, define deliverables. If you can't scope it, you can't price it fixed-fee.
- Test tier pricing against recent engagements to validate profitability. Margins should be consistent across tiers (40-45% is healthy). If a tier is unprofitable, don't offer it.
