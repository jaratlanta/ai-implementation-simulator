## Core Insight

The Medicine, Vitamin, Candy framework is Meaningful AI's prioritization model for categorizing AI initiatives. Medicine is compliance, security, and regulatory requirements — non-negotiable, must-have. Vitamin is personal enablement and productivity — important for long-term health but not urgent. Candy is the exciting AI innovation work — flashy, appealing, but not the place to start. Organizations that start with Candy fail. Organizations that start with Medicine build trust, governance, and a foundation for everything else.

## Context

When organizations begin their AI journey, there's a natural tendency to chase the most exciting possibilities — generative AI for marketing, chatbots for customer service, predictive analytics for sales. These are Candy initiatives. They're appealing but they skip foundational work. The MVC framework forces a conversation about sequencing: what must you do (Medicine), what should you do (Vitamin), and what could you do (Candy)? This reordering dramatically improves success rates because it addresses security and compliance first, builds individual capability second, and pursues innovation third.

## Detail

### Medicine (Must-Have: Compliance & Security)
These are the non-negotiable foundations. Security policies for AI usage, data governance frameworks, compliance with industry regulations (HIPAA, SOX, GDPR, etc.), acceptable use policies, and risk management protocols. If an organization doesn't have these in place, every AI initiative is built on sand. Medicine isn't exciting, but it's what keeps the organization alive and protected.

Examples: AI acceptable use policies, data classification for AI training, security review of AI vendors, regulatory compliance mapping, incident response plans for AI-related breaches.

### Vitamin (Should-Have: Personal Enablement)
Once the foundation is secure, focus shifts to building individual AI capability across the organization. This is Gear 1 work — training people to use AI tools effectively, building confidence, reducing fear, establishing best practices. Vitamins don't solve an immediate crisis, but they build organizational health over time. Skip this phase and you get low adoption, shadow AI, and wasted technology investment.

Examples: Executive AI Immersion Workshops, team-level training programs, prompt engineering best practices, AI tool evaluation and selection, productivity measurement.

### Candy (Could-Have: AI Innovation)
The exciting stuff — custom AI agents, predictive models, automated workflows, AI-native applications. This is where the transformative business value lives, but only if Medicine and Vitamin are already in place. Organizations that jump to Candy without the foundation face security incidents, low adoption, regulatory issues, and failed projects.

Examples: Custom AI agents for sales, AI-powered analytics dashboards, automated document processing, predictive customer models, AI-native software builds.

## Application Guide

1. **Audit:** Categorize all current and proposed AI initiatives as Medicine, Vitamin, or Candy.
2. **Sequence:** Ensure Medicine items are addressed first, Vitamin items are in progress, before committing resources to Candy.
3. **Communicate:** Use the framework to set expectations with leadership — the exciting work comes after the foundational work.
4. **Balance:** Maintain ongoing Medicine and Vitamin work even when Candy initiatives are underway.
5. **Review:** Regularly reassess categorization as the regulatory landscape and organizational maturity evolve.

## Key Takeaways

- Medicine (compliance/security) comes first — it's non-negotiable
- Vitamin (personal enablement) builds the human capability needed for innovation
- Candy (AI innovation) is where the value is, but only on a proper foundation
- Most failed AI initiatives started with Candy and skipped Medicine
- The framework creates a shared language for prioritization conversations
