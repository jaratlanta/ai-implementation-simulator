## Core Insight
Position tiered retainers ($5K/$8K/$12K/month) as having increasing resource embeddedness: starter is project-based, mid-tier allows 2-3 deliverables/month, top-tier positions as a "fractional AI team" with dedicated point of contact and prioritized turnaround. This creates a natural upgrade path based on client maturity and success metrics, driving expansion revenue.

## Context
Most firms sell retainers as flat offerings—"we'll help you with AI" at a fixed price. This approach misses a critical opportunity: clients don't have a stable maturity level. A company starting AI adoption in January looks different by June when they've completed their first three workflows and have early wins. The first company wants guidance and direction; the second wants execution at scale. Without a clear upgrade path, you either leave expansion revenue on the table or create friction by renegotiating the original engagement. The solution is positioning tiers not by hours or features, but by resource embeddedness—how tightly integrated your team becomes in their operations. This reframes pricing as investment in capability, not commodity time.

## Detail

**Tier Structure and Positioning**

**Tier 1: Starter ($5K/month)**
- Positioning: "Project-based support with strategic guidance"
- Resource model: Ad-hoc access to senior strategist (4-8 hours/month) plus templated delivery
- Typical deliverables: 1 major workflow or discovery project per month, or 2-3 smaller process improvements
- Communication: Monthly business review, email support
- Turnaround: 2-3 week delivery cycle
- Outcomes: "Get started with AI automation; understand your highest-impact opportunities"
- Client profile: Early-stage exploration, limited internal AI expertise, bandwidth constraints

**Tier 2: Growth ($8K/month)**
- Positioning: "Dedicated ongoing support for scaling adoption"
- Resource model: One dedicated account lead (12-16 hours/month), templated workflows, integrated roadmapping
- Typical deliverables: 2-3 workflows per month, priority process optimization, integration with existing systems
- Communication: Bi-weekly sync, Slack-based response, 1-hour response SLA for urgent issues
- Turnaround: 1-2 week delivery cycle
- Outcomes: "Build 15-20 automations in 6-12 months; embed AI into core processes"
- Client profile: Active adoption phase, proven internal champions, expanding AI use cases

**Tier 3: Fractional Team ($12K/month)**
- Positioning: "Fractional AI team with embedded decision-making authority"
- Resource model: Dedicated account lead + implementation specialist (24-32 hours/month), first-priority queue, strategic advisory
- Typical deliverables: 4-6 workflows per month, complex integrations, cross-functional optimization, roadmap planning
- Communication: Weekly sync, Slack for daily collaboration, next-business-day response
- Turnaround: 3-5 business days for standard work
- Outcomes: "Scale to 40+ automations in 12 months; become an AI-first organization"
- Client profile: Mature adoption phase, internal AI center of excellence, enterprise-scale ambition

**How Resource Embeddedness Drives Stickiness**

Resource embeddedness is the degree to which your team is integrated into the client's operations. Higher embeddedness creates stickiness because:

1. **Switching Cost Increases**: When your team has attended 15 strategy meetings, understood the organizational context, built relationships across departments, and become a trusted voice in decisions, replacing that is painful. A new vendor has to restart that relationship-building.

2. **Knowledge Lock-In**: At Tier 3, your team understands not just what the client is doing, but *why* decisions were made, what failed in the past, and what political dynamics affect AI adoption. That knowledge lives in your team member, not in a document.

3. **Execution Velocity**: By month 6 in Tier 3, your team is moving 3x faster than they did in month 1 because context is internalized. A new vendor restarts that ramp.

4. **Organizational Dependency**: When your Tier 3 account lead is invited to leadership meetings, asked for opinions on initiatives outside the AI domain, and treated as an advisor (not just a vendor), the organization becomes genuinely dependent on that relationship.

5. **Discovery Cost Elimination**: Each new engagement with a new vendor requires weeks of discovery. Retaining an embedded team bypasses that cost, making continuation a no-brainer financially for the client.

Tier 1 clients are low-stickiness: they're learning and might move on once they understand how to build workflows themselves. Tier 3 clients are high-stickiness: they want a partner, not a tool; an advisor, not a contractor.

**Natural Upgrade Triggers**

Positioning upgrade paths as natural milestones removes sales friction:

- **Successful First Workflow**: "Congratulations on shipping your first automation. As you scale to 3-5 workflows, most companies upgrade to Growth tier to get faster turnaround and a dedicated lead."

- **Third Completed Workflow**: Data point suggesting they've found product-market fit for internal adoption. Now the constraint is execution speed, not strategy.

- **Team Expansion Signal**: "We're bringing in operations staff dedicated to this" signals they're ready for deeper partnership. Pitch Tier 3 as the cost of embedding versus hiring headcount.

- **Expansion to New Department**: Moving from finance automations to HR and operations is a complexity multiplier. Tier 3 ensures consistent quality across new domains.

- **Approaching Threshold Metrics**: If Tier 1 metrics show 80+ hours of realized value monthly, the math shows them that Tier 2 is cheaper than custom project work.

- **Stated Ambition**: "We want to become an AI-first organization" is a Tier 3 conversation. Validate the ambition, then position the fractional team as the way to achieve it.

These triggers are built into your quarterly business reviews. You're not selling the upgrade; you're reflecting the client's own progress back at them.

**Relationship to Gears Model Maturity**

The tier structure aligns with the Gears Model progression:

- **Gear 1** (Awareness): Tier 1 engagement; client is learning what's possible
- **Gear 2** (Adoption): Tier 1 or 2; client is building first workflows, proving value
- **Gear 3** (Scaling): Tier 2 or 3; client is expanding adoption, building organizational muscle
- **Gear 4** (Excellence)**: Tier 3 minimum; client has internal capability and uses external team for strategic/complex work

The tiering positions your role differently at each gear level. At Gear 1, you're a teacher. At Gear 2, you're a builder and guide. At Gear 3, you're an extension of their team. At Gear 4, you're an advisor and specialized capability.

When clients ask "Why does this cost more?", the Gears Model answer is: "Gear 3 and 4 require deeper partnership. You're not paying for more hours; you're paying for faster decision-making, better outcomes, and someone embedded in your culture."

**How This Differs from Traditional Consulting Retainers**

Traditional consulting retainers are time-based: "We'll provide 80 hours per month at $200/hour." They commoditize the provider. Hours become the negotiation point.

This model is outcome and embeddedness-based: "You get a dedicated team member embedded in your operations, first access to our execution capacity, and weekly strategic review." Hours are invisible to the client because the focus is on what they're trying to achieve, not how many hours it takes.

This difference matters because:

1. **Price Negotiation Changes**: Clients don't negotiate "hours per month" because hours aren't the offer. They negotiate "is the outcome worth $X/month?" That's a different conversation—sometimes easier to close.

2. **Utilization Becomes Internal**: If you have excess capacity in Tier 3, that's not the client's problem. You manage it by batching work, creating buffer capacity, or growing the team. The client's cost stays flat.

3. **Expansion is Natural**: Moving from Tier 1 to Tier 2 isn't "we're asking for more hours"; it's "we're increasing your embedded support because you're scaling." Same language as upgrading a subscription.

4. **Retention Focus**: You're not trying to keep the client busy to justify hours. You're trying to create irreplaceability through embeddedness, which is a totally different dynamic.

## Application Guide

1. **Communicate Tiers as Resource Embeddedness, Not Hours**: In early conversations, position Tier 1 as "ad-hoc access," Tier 2 as "dedicated support," and Tier 3 as "become part of our team." Don't mention hours.

2. **Build Upgrade Conversations into BizReview Cadence**: At each quarterly review, reference progress against the Gears Model and position the next tier as the natural investment. Use metrics (workflows built, value realized) to justify the upgrade.

3. **Price Tier 2 and 3 Based on Client Outcomes, Not Incremental Cost**: If Tier 1 is $5K and your incremental cost for Tier 2 is $2K, don't price at $7K. Price at $8K to reflect the expanded scope and smoother execution. Reserve 20-30% margin cushion for expansion tier offerings.

4. **Assign Dedicated Account Leads for Tier 2 and 3**: This is non-negotiable. The account lead is your primary stickiness mechanism. Choose carefully—look for people who can operate comfortably at C-level, understand client business context, and build genuine relationships.

5. **Create Clear Scope Boundaries by Tier**: Document what "included" means at each tier. This prevents scope creep in Tier 1 and creates clear cases for Tier 3 when work exceeds boundaries.

6. **Track Upgrade Path KPIs**: Monitor time-to-upgrade, % of clients upgrading, and reasons for declining upgrades. This data tells you whether your tier positioning is working.

7. **Use Tier 3 as Your Flagship**: Make Tier 3 your show-stopper offering. It's where margins are best, stickiness is highest, and impact is deepest. Everything else is a path to Tier 3.

## Key Takeaways
- Tiering based on resource embeddedness creates natural upgrade paths and drives expansion revenue
- Tier 1 is low-stickiness (client learns and may leave); Tier 3 is high-stickiness (team becomes organizational fixture)
- Upgrade triggers should be based on client maturity, not arbitrary timelines
- The tier structure aligns naturally with Gears Model progression (Awareness → Adoption → Scaling → Excellence)
- Embedding creates switching costs; make this the foundation of your retention strategy
- Tier 3 (fractional team model) is your highest-margin, highest-value offering—position it as the flagship
