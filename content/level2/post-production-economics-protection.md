## Core Insight
When structuring partnerships where you build intellectual property that will then be monetized through end-customers, ensure the partnership agreement locks in your economics on per-user revenue before partners begin selling to third parties. This prevents renegotiation after customer acquisition and protects your investment.

## Context
Many partnership models involve one party investing significant resources to build IP, workflows, or integrations that a partner then sells to their own customer base. The critical vulnerability emerges after the partner succeeds in acquisition: once customers are locked in and adoption is proven, partners frequently attempt to renegotiate economics downward, claiming the product "wasn't as valuable as expected" or requesting volume discounts that retroactively reduce your returns. This dynamic has destroyed value in numerous healthcare platform integrations, SaaS partnerships, and cross-industry licensing arrangements. The problem is structural: the negotiation timing is asymmetric. During pre-build discussions, partners have limited leverage. Once they've sold to 50+ customers who now depend on your IP, they have substantial leverage to demand better terms.

## Detail
**The Economics Lock Timing Problem**

The fundamental issue is that partnership agreements typically specify pricing structures with language like "to be negotiated" or "market-based pricing" rather than committing to specific unit economics upfront. Partners view this flexibility as prudent—they don't know if the market will bear certain price points. However, this flexibility becomes a renegotiation weapon once they've achieved market validation.

**Why Post-Acquisition Renegotiation Happens**

Partners renegotiate for several predictable reasons:
- They've proven market demand and reduce uncertainty, so they believe they should get better terms
- Their sales teams, once successful, now demand margin expansion to hit internal targets
- They've built customer relationships and feel they can pressure suppliers who've already invested
- Your IP becomes embedded in their customer contracts, creating switching costs that work against you
- They discover they want to build competing capabilities and use renegotiation as leverage to prevent competition

**How to Structure Protection Clauses**

Effective protection requires locking economics into the contract *before* build begins:

1. **Per-Unit Revenue Share with Escalation, Not Discounting**: Instead of a fixed margin per customer, structure as "X% of customer annual contract value" with escalation bands (e.g., 15% on revenue $0-$100K, 18% on revenue $100K-$500K). This aligns your upside with their success and removes the discount-negotiation weapon.

2. **Minimum Volume Commitments**: Require the partner to guarantee minimum transaction volumes (e.g., "$500K aggregate monthly revenue or $50K monthly guarantee") that survive a specified term (typically 3-5 years). This prevents them from stalling rollout post-acquisition while you've already invested.

3. **Most-Favored-Nations (MFN) Clauses**: Include language preventing them from paying lower rates for equivalent IP to competing suppliers. This prevents them from building competitive alternatives and then claiming your IP should match lower market rates.

4. **Automatic Renewal with Renegotiation Windows**: Lock the economic terms for a primary commitment period (3 years), with any renegotiation only allowed in defined windows (e.g., year 3, year 5). Outside those windows, terms auto-renew unchanged. This prevents continuous pressure.

5. **IP Ownership and License Scope Clarity**: Specify exactly what IP is licensed and for what purposes. If they want to build competing capabilities, they must negotiate separate terms. Prevent the "we built this ourselves" reinterpretation.

**Relationship Between Build Investment and Ongoing Economics**

The investment you make in building partnership IP must be proportional to your protection mechanisms. If you're investing $200K in build, lock economics for at least 18-24 months of predictable revenue. If you're building a complete platform integration, lock for 36+ months. Partners will push back on long lock-ins, but they're reasonable when framed around your build investment. Present it as: "We're investing $X in this; we need Y months of stable economics to achieve ROI."

**Examples of What Goes Wrong Without Protection**

Without upfront economic locks:
- A healthcare platform builds a specialized workflow for a partner; partner acquires 60 customers in year 1, then renegotiates pricing down 40%, claiming "market rates are lower"
- A SaaS company builds a native integration; partner's sales team, now successful, demands margin expansion in year 2, threatening to replace the integration with a built-in alternative
- A data integration partner agrees to resell your IP; once embedded, they claim your pricing doesn't align with their volume discounts and demand a "partnership adjustment"

With proper protection, these conversations are prevented at the contract level rather than happening after customer acquisition.

## Application Guide
1. **Early in Partnership Discussions**: Present a draft economics structure before build begins. Be explicit that you need economic certainty before investing. Partners may push back, but framing it as proportional to your investment is reasonable and expected.

2. **Use Tiered Economics to Your Advantage**: Rather than a flat per-user fee, use escalating revenue-share tiers that give both parties upside. This makes the contract less about discount negotiation and more about shared success.

3. **Document All IP Assumptions**: Create a detailed specification of what constitutes "licensed IP" versus what the partner builds themselves. This prevents later arguments about ownership boundaries.

4. **Escalate Partner Executive Buy-In**: Get the partner's CFO and business lead to sign off on the economics, not just the product team. Ensures institutional commitment beyond whoever originally negotiated.

5. **Build Renegotiation Triggers into the Contract**: Rather than preventing all renegotiation, allow defined moments (e.g., after 18 months of operation) where if volumes significantly exceed expectations, both parties share the upside. This is less adversarial than "never renegotiate" and often leads to partnership extensions.

## Key Takeaways
- Economics must be locked *before* build, not after customer acquisition proves viability
- Per-unit revenue-share models with escalation are more durable than flat fees, as they align incentives
- Minimum volume commitments and MFN clauses are essential protection mechanisms for protecting your ROI
- The build investment size should determine the length and strength of your economic protection
- Without upfront locks, successful partnerships become renegotiation opportunities for partners once switching costs materialize
