## Core Insight
For multi-tenant SaaS platforms serving facility-based industries (healthcare, long-term care, hospitality), revenue can be structured as PEPM (per-employee-per-month) for end customers, while partnership deals with anchor clients are structured as fixed-fee build investments with potential future revenue sharing. This dual-layer pricing strategy separates go-to-market mechanics from partnership structure, allowing the company to grow predictable recurring revenue while strategically deepening relationships with marquee customers.

## Context
Facility-based industries have two distinct pricing challenges: (1) standardized, scalable revenue from numerous small-to-medium facilities, and (2) concentrated deals with large anchor customers who want custom builds and negotiated terms. Attempting to use a single pricing model creates tension: either the anchor customer subsidizes margin-low standard customers, or PEPM pricing is so high that it prevents adoption at smaller facilities.

The dual-structure approach emerged from observing that successful SaaS platforms in healthcare and workforce management separate their growth motion (land-and-expand through PEPM adoption) from their strategic motion (deep partnerships with large health systems, long-term care chains, or hospitality groups). This allows pricing that's fair to both segments while protecting unit economics on both sides.

## Detail

### PEPM Mechanics
Per-Employee-Per-Month pricing ties cost directly to the facility's headcount. The end customer pays a fixed rate per employee per month, usually with volume discounts at different tiers.

**Typical Structure**:
- 0-50 employees: $8/employee/month
- 51-150 employees: $6/employee/month
- 151-500 employees: $4/employee/month
- 501+ employees: Custom negotiation (often flat fee)

**Why PEPM works for facility-based industries**:
- **Transparent scaling**: A facility with 80 employees knows exactly what they'll pay, and when they add 20 staff, cost scales predictably.
- **Facility parity**: A 50-person clinic and a 100-person clinic in different regions pay based on the same metric, creating fairness.
- **Usage alignment**: More employees usually means more usage, so PEPM captures some of that correlation without complex usage metering.
- **Adoption velocity**: PEPM removes the biggest barrier to adoption—facilities know they're not paying for unneeded capacity.

**Danger zone**: Setting PEPM too high kills adoption at small facilities; too low, and you can't afford to serve them. Volume discounts help, but the base pricing tier is critical.

### Why Separation of Pricing Layers Matters
A single, inflexible pricing model forces bad trade-offs:

**Scenario A: Anchor customer at PEPM rates**
An anchor customer with 1,500 employees at $4/PEPM = $72,000/year. If they want a custom feature (integration with legacy system, white-label option, dedicated support), you either:
- Absorb the cost in margin (unsustainable if many anchors want this)
- Demand a premium (they push back: "but we're paying PEPM like everyone else")
- Tie the custom feature to higher PEPM (misaligns pricing with value, creates resentment)

**Scenario B: Standard customer structure dictates anchor pricing**
If you build pricing to accommodate anchor customization, your entry-level PEPM becomes unaffordable for small facilities, limiting total addressable market.

**Separation solves this**: Anchor customers are treated as **strategic partnerships**, not SaaS customers. Their deal structure is:
- Fixed upfront fee for platform build/customization (e.g., $150k-$500k depending on scope)
- Transition to PEPM once live (or alternative: fixed annual fee with revenue share if outcomes align)
- Potential upside share if the partnership drives referrals or network effects

This creates three distinct advantages:

1. **Margin clarity**: Build costs are sunk upfront (or amortized); PEPM covers ongoing service delivery. You know your service margin on the base product.

2. **PEPM sustainability**: Your standard PEPM pricing can be aggressive (good for adoption) because you're not cross-subsidizing anchor customization.

3. **Partnership optionality**: With anchors, you can negotiate terms (revenue share, referral fees, volume discounts) that would destroy PEPM mechanics if applied universally.

### When to Use Fixed-Fee vs. Revenue Share

**Fixed-Fee Build Model** (best for most partnerships):
- $150k-$500k upfront for custom development, integration, training, and go-live
- Platform PEPM begins once live
- Usually 12-24 month initial commitment
- Clear, simple economics; partner knows their total cost

**When to use**: Anchor customer wants customization but isn't willing to tie economics to outcomes. You have confidence in your ability to deliver results. Early partnership where you want to prove value before sharing risk.

**Revenue Share Model** (best for high-upside partnerships):
- Smaller or zero upfront fee for build
- Partnership kicks in after platform goes live: you share in the margin improvement, cost reduction, or revenue upside the platform generates
- Usually 20-40% share of incremental value for 2-3 years
- High variability but high upside

**When to use**: Large anchor customer where you're confident the platform will drive significant value. Partner is more sophisticated and wants alignment. You can afford to absorb build risk. Long-term, strategic partner where optionality matters more than short-term cash.

**Hybrid Approach** (increasingly common):
- Modest upfront fee ($50k-$150k) for build and go-live
- Small revenue share for 18-24 months post-launch (10-20%)
- Caps on total revenue share (e.g., max $50k/year)
- Transitions to PEPM once revenue share period ends

This balances immediate cash recovery with upside participation. Anchors feel the company is "in it with them" while you get paid for the build work.

### Protecting Margins on Both Sides

The biggest risk in dual-pricing models is margin compression on one side or the other.

**Risk 1: PEPM customers subsidize anchor implementation**
If your service delivery team is stretched thin, they might cut corners on PEPM customers to handle anchor builds. This degrades PEPM retention and NPS while anchor work looks profitable.

**Mitigation**: Separate service delivery teams (or at least separate project budgets) for anchors vs. PEPM. PEPM customers should have predictable, high-margin support. Anchor implementations should be ring-fenced to a delivery team with realistic capacity.

**Risk 2: Anchor deal structure becomes the template**
Once you do one revenue share deal, every subsequent anchor expects the same terms. Your sales team starts treating revenue share as standard rather than exceptional.

**Mitigation**: Document anchor deal terms as "strategic partnership," not "SaaS." Make the distinction explicit in contracting. Price anchors aggressively on fixed fees; use revenue share sparingly for truly strategic deals.

**Risk 3: PEPM compression to "win" competitive deals**
A prospect pushes back on PEPM; sales offers a "special rate." Over time, these accumulate and your average PEPM decays.

**Mitigation**: Set PEPM floors (e.g., never below $3/employee/month). Use other levers to compete: longer trial periods, integration support, onboarding hours, not pricing. If a customer can't afford standard PEPM, they may not be a good fit.

**Risk 4: Anchor profitability hidden by allocation issues**
If you use a single P&L for anchors and PEPM customers, poor anchor margins can hide in overhead allocation. You think you're profitable when you're not.

**Mitigation**: Run separate P&Ls for PEPM (standard, recurring) and Partnerships (projects, variable). Track contribution margin separately. Anchor profitability should be explicitly calculated, not buried.

## Application Guide

**Step 1: Validate PEPM baseline**
Before launching PEPM, test it with 3-5 pilot customers across different size cohorts. Confirm that PEPM pricing is:
- Affordable for small facilities (your entry market)
- Defensible for large facilities (where margin risk is highest)
- Aligned with value delivered (roughly: does a facility with 2x employees get 2x value?)

**Step 2: Establish anchor criteria**
Define what makes a customer "anchor" vs. "PEPM standard." Typical criteria:
- Employee count (e.g., 500+ employees)
- Revenue size (e.g., $50M+ annual revenue)
- Strategic importance (key reference, network effect, geographic expansion)
- Custom requirements (need platform integration beyond standard offering)

Being explicit prevents sales from treating all large customers as anchors and negotiating PEPM heavily.

**Step 3: Build anchor deal templates**
Create standard term sheets for:
- Fixed-fee build (scope, timeline, cost, what's included)
- Post-go-live PEPM (if applicable)
- Revenue share (if applicable): what counts as incremental value, how is it calculated, what's the time period?

Templates reduce negotiation friction while protecting your position.

**Step 4: Implement operational separation**
- Separate sales process: PEPM sales team moves customers self-serve; anchor deals go through executive/partnership sales
- Separate delivery: PEPM gets standard onboarding (2-3 weeks); anchor gets custom implementation (8-12 weeks)
- Separate P&Ls: track PEPM and partnership revenue separately so you can see profitability by segment

**Step 5: Price anchors conservatively**
First anchor deals often under-price because you're trading cash for reference/learning. Budget for this loss leader, but don't let it become standard.

Typical anchor build deal: $250k-$400k fixed fee for first major customer, $100k-$150k for subsequent partners (you have templates, less custom work).

**Step 6: Monitor PEPM health and anchor expansion path**
Track:
- PEPM adoption rate (are new facilities adopting?)
- PEPM net retention (are existing customers staying and expanding headcount?)
- PEPM churn (especially to free/competitive offerings)
- Anchor contribution margin (PEPM revenue minus service delivery cost)

Use this data to adjust PEPM pricing, improve service delivery, and decide when to make a new anchor deal.

## Key Takeaways
- PEPM pricing is appropriate for multi-tenant SaaS in facility-based industries because it aligns cost with a clear facility metric (headcount) and enables adoption at all facility sizes.
- Anchor partnerships should be priced as strategic initiatives (fixed-fee builds + optional PEPM/revenue share), not as high-margin PEPM deals. This separation protects PEPM margins and allows partnership flexibility.
- Fixed-fee build structures work best for most partnerships; reserve revenue share for high-upside strategic deals where you can afford implementation risk and want long-term alignment.
- Dual-pricing models require operational separation: distinct sales motions, distinct delivery teams, distinct P&Ls. If you try to serve PEPM and anchors with the same team/process, margins collapse on one side.
- The biggest risk is PEPM compression through competitive discounting. Use non-price levers to compete; protect PEPM floors to maintain sustainable unit economics.
